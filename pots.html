• Material Notes: Usually large, often stainless steel or aluminum. Used for soups, stocks, boiling pasta.

    1. Rinse immediately after use to remove loose food.
    2. Fill with warm soapy water and soak if there are stuck-on bits.
    3. Use a non-abrasive sponge; for stubborn spots make a paste of baking soda + water and scrub gently.
    4. Rinse well and dry to prevent water spots.
    5. If stainless steel has discoloration, boil a mixture of water + vinegar, then wash and dry.

  • Material Notes: Medium-sized, usually stainless steel, aluminum, or non-stick. Good for sauces, reheating, small boiling tasks.

    1. Rinse and fill with warm soapy water right after use.
    2. For burned-on sauce, simmer water + a tablespoon of baking soda for 10–15 minutes, let cool, then scrub.
    3. Avoid abrasive pads on non-stick surfaces; use soft sponge only.
    4. For stuck food on stainless, sprinkle baking soda and scrub with a damp sponge.
    5. Dry thoroughly to avoid water spots or corrosion.

  • Material Notes: Comes in non-stick, stainless steel, or cast iron varieties. Used for frying, sautéing.

    1. Non-stick: wipe with paper towel to remove excess oil, then wash with warm soapy water and a soft sponge. Do NOT use metal utensils or abrasive cleaners.
    2. Stainless: soak briefly, scrub with non-abrasive pad; for stubborn brown bits, use baking soda paste.
    3. Cast iron: while still warm, wipe out excess oil/food, rinse with hot water (no soap if possible), scrub with brush or coarse salt. Dry immediately and apply a thin layer of oil to maintain seasoning.
    4. Never put seasoned cast iron in dishwasher.

  • Material Notes: Often heavy cast iron (enameled or bare). Great for braising, stews, slow-cooking.

    1. Enameled: soak in warm soapy water, use soft sponge; for stains use a paste of baking soda or a dash of cream of tartar.
    2. Bare cast iron: clean like other cast iron — hot water, coarse salt scrub if needed, dry and re-oil.
    3. Avoid thermal shock (don't run cold water over a hot enameled pot).
    4. Remove food residue with gentle scraping before washing.

  • Material Notes: Wider, straight sides; commonly stainless steel or non-stick. Used for searing and pan sauces.

    1. Rinse and soak if necessary to loosen residues.
    2. Use warm soapy water and a non-abrasive sponge; for fond (browned bits) deglaze with water/vinegar then wash.
    3. Non-stick: avoid high-scratch tools and harsh scouring pads.
    4. Polish stainless occasionally with a gentle stainless cleaner to remove streaks.

  • Material Notes: Usually carbon steel or cast iron; lightweight carbon steel woks develop seasoning and are preferred for stir-frying.

    1. After cooking, rinse with hot water and use a soft brush or sponge to remove food (avoid soap on seasoned carbon steel).
    2. For stuck-on food, simmer water in the wok to loosen residue, then scrape gently.
    3. Dry immediately and apply light oil to prevent rust.
    4. If rust appears, scrub with steel wool down to bare metal, re-season by coating with oil and heating.

  • Material Notes: Often stainless steel; includes a perforated insert for steaming vegetables, fish, dumplings.

    1. Rinse away food remnants immediately after use.
    2. Soak if starches stuck to the holes; use a soft brush to clear perforations.
    3. Wash with warm soapy water and rinse thoroughly to remove trapped bits.
    4. Dry completely to prevent water spots and mineral deposits.

  • Material Notes: Inner pot is usually stainless steel or aluminum; lid has rubber gasket and valves that require special care.

    1. Remove gasket and valves (if removable) and wash separately in warm soapy water.
    2. Wash the inner pot with a soft sponge; for burnt food, soak with warm water and baking soda.
    3. Inspect and clean steam vent and sealing ring; replace gasket if warped or cracked.
    4. Dry all parts thoroughly and reassemble according to manufacturer instructions.

  • Material Notes: Can be ceramic, enameled cast iron, or stainless steel. Ideal for slow-cooking, roasting, and one-pot meals.

    1. Allow to cool slightly, then soak in warm soapy water to loosen baked-on food.
    2. Enameled or ceramic: use non-abrasive sponge; for stains use a baking soda paste or soak with warm water + vinegar.
    3. Avoid sudden temperature changes to protect glaze or enamel.
    4. Dry fully before storing to avoid musty smells.

  • Material Notes: Often stainless steel or heavy-duty aluminized steel; used for roasting meats and vegetables. May include a rack.

    1. Scrape away large food bits and grease while still warm (careful — not too hot to handle).
    2. Soak in hot soapy water; for tough grease use a grease-cutting dish soap and a scrubber safe for the material.
    3. For burnt residue, sprinkle baking soda and add hot water; let sit then scrub gently.
    4. Rinse and dry completely; treat stainless surfaces gently to avoid scratching.
